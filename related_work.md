Evaluating Pixel-Space Adversarial Vulnerabilities in Multimodal LLMs

Introduction:

Multimodal large language models (MLLMs) and vision-language models (VLMs) now perform open-ended reasoning over joint image-text inputs, with models such as LLaVA-1.5 serving as strong baselines for visual instruction following [1]. Yet their safety properties are inherited only imperfectly from text-only alignment. Reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF) substantially improves instruction following and reduces harmful behavior in large language models [2], but once a continuous visual channel is fused into generation, the model acquires an additional attack surface that text-only safety alignment does not directly secure [3,4].

This concern is well grounded in the adversarial-example literature. Small, carefully optimized perturbations can alter model behavior even when the input appears nearly unchanged to human observers [5,6]. FGSM provides the canonical one-step linearized attack [6], while PGD has become the standard strong first-order adversary for norm-bounded robustness evaluation [7]. For multimodal safety, however, attack success must be interpreted jointly with visual stealth. LPIPS is especially relevant because it was introduced as a perceptual similarity metric that better matches human judgments than simple pixelwise distances, making it more informative than reporting only PSNR- or SSIM-style distortion when the practical question is whether a successful attack remains visually inconspicuous [8]. 

Recent multimodal safety benchmarks show that the problem is not hypothetical. MM-SafetyBench introduced 5,040 image-text pairs spanning 13 safety-critical scenarios and showed that even safety-aligned MLLMs can be compromised by maliciously constructed image inputs [9]. The survey by Liu et al. synthesizes the emerging literature on evaluation, attack, and defense for MLLM safety on images and text [10]. More recent benchmark work broadens the evaluation lens further: MMJ-Bench argues that prior comparisons were fragmented across datasets and metrics and proposes a unified pipeline for comparing jailbreak attacks and defenses [11], while MMSafeAware shows that current MLLMs often fail in both directions, missing genuinely unsafe multimodal inputs while also over-refusing benign cases [12]. Complementing these benchmarks, ECSO provides a useful mechanistic clue: safety behavior inherited from the underlying LLM is not necessarily absent in MLLMs, but can be suppressed by the introduction of image features [4]. 

Attack papers make this vulnerability concrete. Qi et al. show that visual adversarial examples can jailbreak aligned language-and-vision systems [3]. Jailbreak in Pieces demonstrates that cross-modality attacks can pair adversarial images with otherwise generic textual prompts to defeat alignment [13]. Image Hijacks generalizes the threat beyond safety refusal, showing that adversarial images can steer generative behavior at runtime, leak contextual information, and override safety training [14]. Other recent methods pursue more universal or more scalable jailbreaks through image jailbreak prompts or joint bi-modal optimization across text and vision [15,16]. Taken together, these results establish that the visual channel is not a peripheral nuisance variable; it is a direct control surface over model behavior. 

Nonetheless, an important scientific gap remains. A large fraction of the multimodal jailbreak literature studies query-relevant images, universal image prompts, or jointly optimized text-image attacks [9,13,15,16]. These settings are indispensable for red-teaming, but they entangle two different questions: whether the model is responding to high-level malicious semantics present in the image, and whether low-level pixel-space sensitivity alone is sufficient to break safety alignment. Recent transferability evidence sharpens this concern. Schaeffer et al. conduct a large-scale study over more than 40 open-parameter VLMs and find that transferable gradient-based universal image jailbreaks are surprisingly hard to obtain [17]. That result does not diminish the importance of white- or gray-box attacks; rather, it strengthens the case for carefully controlled per-model baselines that measure what a standard first-order adversary can and cannot do under strict distortion budgets.

Against this backdrop, the question posed in the present project is narrow by design and foundational in value: if the harmful text prompt is held fixed, the base image is benign and natural, and only the pixel values are allowed to change within a small norm-bounded budget, can the model be pushed from refusal toward unsafe compliance? In the proposed study, this question is instantiated on LLaVA-1.5-7B with PGD, clean/noise/FGSM controls, high-severity MM-SafetyBench prompts, and evaluation of attack success rate (ASR) as a function of LPIPS-constrained distortion. This is not merely a convenient baseline: it tests whether multimodal alignment fails at the perceptual input manifold itself, as distinct from failures induced by semantically malicious images or multimodal prompt engineering. Either outcome is informative. Successful attacks at low perceptual distortion would show that multimodal safety remains fragile at the sensory interface itself, whereas failure under tight budgets would place a meaningful lower bound on the practical severity of pixel-space safety vulnerabilities. In either case, mapping the ASR-distortion frontier provides the rigorous baseline that more complex latent-space, universal, or black-box attacks should be compared against.
